Read This Carefully

More Than 99% Are Not Doing This

In any given year, the overwhelming majority of every racial group in New York City is not arrested for murder, rape, robbery, or kidnapping. This page is not an indictment of communities. It is an indictment of policies built on a story the data does not support.

"This isn't about condemning a group.
It's about condemning a narrative that gets people killed."

In New York, the 2019 Bail Elimination Act ended cash bail for most misdemeanors and nonviolent felonies — its sponsor justified it as ending the incarceration of "indigent defendants, predominantly from minority communities." Manhattan DA Alvin Bragg's "Day One" memo in 2022 directed prosecutors to stop charging fare evasion, resisting arrest, trespassing, and obstructing governmental administration, and to downgrade certain robberies. Both passed under a racial-equity framing the underlying arrest data does not support.

The Obvious Objection

"But Isn't It Just Poverty?"

By two separate New York City measures — the Mayor's True Cost of Living and Columbia University's Poverty Tracker — Asian, Hispanic, and Black New Yorkers face economic hardship at similar (or in Hispanics' case, higher) rates. The arrest data does not follow that pattern.

If economic hardship were the driver of violent crime, the data would have a clear pattern. Hispanic New Yorkers face the highest poverty rate on both measures — 77.6% TCOL and 26% federal — and outnumber Black New Yorkers in NYC's population (29% vs 22%). By the poverty hypothesis, Hispanics should account for the largest share of murder, rape, and robbery arrests in the city. They don't. Black New Yorkers lead every violent crime category in the charts above despite having a smaller population and similar (or lower) poverty rates than the Hispanic community. The only category where the two are close is kidnapping. Material deprivation alone does not predict the arrest data.

Why This Matters for the Data Above

Murder has the highest clearance rate, which is why the suspect and arrestee data on the murder chart are nearly identical — most murders end in an arrest, so the two columns capture the same population. Robbery has a much lower clearance rate, which is why suspect and arrestee data diverge for robbery — the suspect descriptions reflect what victims and witnesses reported in the moment, while the arrestee column only captures the ~30% of cases that ended in a booking. In every category, the arrestee data understates the total population of people committing the crime. The full picture is somewhere between the suspect column and the arrestee column — closer to suspect for low-clearance crimes, closer to arrestee for high-clearance ones. Conviction data, which is not in NYPD's reports, would understate even further: only ~3% of reported felonies in NYC end in a felony conviction.

The Other Obvious Objection

"But Isn't It Just Racist Police?"

If the arrest disparity were caused by a racially biased police force, we'd expect to find a department that's overwhelmingly white. We don't. The majority of NYPD officers are people of color, and the largest single group is Hispanic. The same department, with the same demographic makeup, produces the arrest data shown above.

The NYPD is 60.8% people of color. Hispanic officers alone (32.6%) outnumber White officers (39.2% — and even those are a plurality, not a majority). Black officers (16.6%) and Asian officers (11.4%) round out a department where the majority of the people making arrests share a racial or ethnic background with the communities they patrol. This is not a 1960s Bull Connor police force. It's a department whose own demographic composition makes the simple "racist police are inflating Black arrest numbers" story very hard to defend. Whatever explains the arrest patterns shown above, a homogeneously white police force is not it — because that's not what the NYPD is.

The Third Obvious Objection

"But Isn't It Just Historical Discrimination?"

The strongest version of the racial-equity argument is that present-day disparities are downstream of slavery and centuries of structural racism. The data on this page does not require denying that history to challenge the explanation. It requires asking whether the present-day patterns actually match what the historical-discrimination hypothesis would predict. They don't.

If centuries of slavery and structural racism were the primary mechanism driving today's arrest disparities, we would expect to see four specific patterns in the data. We see none of them.

1. THE HISPANIC PROBLEM

Hispanic New Yorkers face the worst hardship of any group by Mayor Mamdani's own True Cost of Living measure: 77.6% cannot meet TCOL, the highest rate in the city. But the ancestors of NYC's Hispanic population were overwhelmingly not enslaved in the United States. The community is largely composed of post-1965 immigrants from the Caribbean, Central America, South America, and the Dominican Republic. If U.S. slavery's legacy were the primary causal mechanism, Hispanic and Black patterns should diverge sharply. They don't. Both groups show elevated arrest shares relative to population. The historical-discrimination hypothesis predicts a difference that the data does not contain.

2. THE ASIAN PROBLEM

Asian Americans experienced significant historical discrimination in the United States — the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, Japanese internment during World War II, decades of redlining and exclusionary housing covenants, anti-Asian violence ongoing into the present day. By Mamdani's TCOL measure, 63.3% of Asian and Pacific Islander New Yorkers cannot meet the cost of living — a hardship rate higher than the White rate by nearly twenty points. If historical discrimination produced present-day crime disparities through any direct mechanism, Asian arrest shares should be elevated. Instead, Asian New Yorkers show significantly lower-than-population arrest shares across every category on this page. Whatever mechanism is producing the patterns above, it is not "historical discrimination causes elevated crime."

3. THE WITHIN-GROUP PROBLEM

NYC's Black population is not monolithic. It includes descendants of U.S. slavery, post-emancipation Great Migration arrivals, recent Caribbean immigrants (Jamaican, Haitian, Trinidadian, Guyanese, Bajan), and direct West and East African immigrants. These subgroups have radically different historical relationships to U.S. slavery, and the limited research that has disaggregated them finds substantial outcome differences. The racial-equity framework collapses these into a single analytical category and treats all members as if they share the same historical trajectory. When the explanatory variable is collapsed but the outcomes inside the category are heterogeneous, the explanation is not doing the work it claims to be doing.

4. THE PRESENT-TENSE PROBLEM

The page does not require resolving any historical question. The data shown is from 2008 through 2024, and the framing is comparative across currently existing populations. The question is not "why does history have the shape it has." The question is whether present-day disparities are explained by present-day hardship, present-day policing bias, or any single causal story. The answer the data gives is no. None of those single stories — slavery, poverty, racist police — predicts the patterns the city's own numbers actually show. The strongest position is to stop pretending any of them does.

None of the above denies that slavery happened, that historical discrimination shaped present-day economic geography, or that past policy created persistent inequality. All of those are real. The argument is narrower and more careful: the specific mechanism by which "historical discrimination causes today's violent crime arrest patterns" doesn't survive contact with how the patterns actually distribute across groups with different historical trajectories. Acknowledging history is not the same thing as accepting any specific causal story about how history determines the present.

What This Means

Bad Data Becomes Bad Policy

When the empirical premise of a policy doesn't survive contact with the data, the policy doesn't survive contact with reality either, and the people most harmed by the gap are usually the people the policy claimed to protect.

Case in Point · April 2026

A 7-month-old was killed by a gang bullet in Brooklyn.
The Mayor wants to abolish the tool used to catch the killer.

On April 1, 2026, 7-month-old Kaori Patterson-Moore was shot and killed in her stroller in East Williamsburg, Brooklyn. A 21-year-old gang associate fired from the back of a moped at a rival from a different NYCHA complex. The bullet hit the baby in the head. NYPD identified the shooter and his accomplice within 48 hours.

They did it using the NYPD gang database.
— Confirmed by Commissioner Tisch on the record

At the press conference for his Racial Equity Plan on April 7, a reporter asked Mayor Mamdani about it directly. The exchange is on the record in the official transcript:

REPORTER: "Last week, Commissioner Tisch said that she's using the gang database on a daily basis and particularly used it to help apprehend the suspects in [Williamsburg], or the shooting that happened with the seven-month-old baby last week. I just wanted to get, what is your standpoint on her using the gang database on a regular basis?"

MAYOR MAMDANI: "So, I've made my critiques of the database clear, and the NYPD has also implemented a number of reforms as per the recommendation that came through. And the implementation of those reforms and the results of that are part of the active discussion that we're having."

The Mayor was asked whether the gang database should be used to apprehend the killers of a 7-month-old Black infant. He did not say yes. He did not even acknowledge the case. He restated his "critiques" and called for further "discussion."

Mamdani campaigned on abolishing the NYPD's Criminal Group Database, calling it a "vast dragnet". Privacy advocates in his orbit have openly pressured him to dismantle it by executive order. The objection to the database is that 98% of the people in it are Black or Hispanic, and that this proportion is itself proof of racial profiling.

But the data on this page shows the same 98% pattern in arrestees, suspects, AND victims for the violent crimes the database is used to investigate. The database is racially skewed because the underlying violent gang activity it tracks is racially skewed in exactly the same direction, and the people most harmed by that activity are the same people the database is accused of profiling. Kaori Patterson-Moore was not a statistic. She was a 7-month-old Black infant killed by gang violence in a Black and Hispanic neighborhood, and the tool the Mayor wants to abolish is the tool that helped find her killer.

"The policies built on the false framing harm Black New Yorkers most.
The data shows they are disproportionately the victims —
and the racial equity plan would take away the tools used to protect them."

At the press conference releasing his Racial Equity Plan on April 6, 2026, Mayor Mamdani argued that NYC's racial disparities "are not inevitable. They are the result of policy choices." That sentence is the empirical premise this entire page was built to test, using the Mayor's own data.

The premise that disparities are "the result of policy choices" is the foundation underneath both the Racial Equity Plan and the broader criminal-justice rollback the city is currently engaged in. If the premise is correct, both policy directions follow. If the premise is wrong, both are weakened. The page tests the premise the only honest way it can be tested: against the data the city itself publishes.

New York's recent criminal justice reforms — the 2019 Bail Elimination Act, Manhattan DA Alvin Bragg's 2022 Day One memo, and the broader push to reduce arrests, prosecutions, and pretrial detention — were sold under a specific empirical claim: that the system was punishing poor people of color for being poor, and that the disparities in arrest data reflected biased enforcement of a system that should be rolled back rather than the underlying distribution of the crime itself.

This page is a test of that empirical claim. Every chart on it uses NYPD's own data, the Mayor's own data, Columbia University's own data, and NYC's own demographic data. When you put those primary sources next to each other, the claim does not hold up:

  • Black New Yorkers are disproportionately the victims of violent crime, not just the arrestees
  • Poverty does not predict the arrest pattern — Hispanic New Yorkers face higher poverty by both NYC measures and have a larger total population, yet account for a smaller share of arrests across every category
  • Equally-sized populations facing equal hardship produce arrest counts that differ by an order of magnitude
  • A police force that is 60.8% people of color produces the same arrest patterns the framing claims a "racist police force" produces — meaning the framing is wrong about something important

Mayor Mamdani's True Cost of Living measure is genuinely useful data, and we used it on this page. But the policy framework being built on top of it — that the disparities in NYC criminal justice are downstream of poverty, that more leniency will reduce harm, that the criminal justice system is the problem rather than the response to a problem — is the same framework the data on this page does not support. The Mayor's poverty data is real. The narrative built on top of it is not.

Policies built on faulty empirical premises don't fail quietly. They fail on the people the false premise was supposed to protect. The 2019 bail reform was sold as helping poor minority communities. The communities it was supposed to help are the same communities most disproportionately victimized by the violent crime the reform made easier to commit without consequence. That's not a slogan. That's the data on this page.

What this page argues for is amendment, not abandonment, of these policies — built on the data that actually exists rather than the data the framing wishes existed. The empirical basis matters. Get the empirical basis wrong and every policy downstream of it gets the people downstream of it wrong too.

Methodology

Murder, rape, robbery — arrestee data: From NYPD annual Enforcement Report Tables. Counts = proportion × known-race total. Arrestee race/ethnicity is recorded by the booking officer based on identification documents and physical assessment.

Kidnapping — arrestee data: From NYPD Arrests Data (Historic) on NYC Open Data. Filtered for "Kidnapping & Related Offenses."

Race categories: Black, Hispanic, White, Asian/Other. Asian/Other = Asian/Pacific Islander + American Indian. For kidnapping, "BLACK HISPANIC"/"WHITE HISPANIC" → Hispanic; "UNKNOWN"/"OTHER" excluded.

Data correction: 2009 Rape American Indian decimal-shift error in source corrected.
